How to Play

The Game

Druid's Duel is a physical game of Celtic knot weaving played with a set of five tile types — Loops, Spans, Bends, Branches, and Crosses. Each player draws their tiles from a cloth pouch and uses them to weave intricate, closed-path knots on the table in front of them.

Solo Play

Select a challenge using the Play page. The challenge will specify exactly how many of each weave type to use. Empty your pouch, select the specified tiles, and race against the clock to weave a complete closed knot — no loose ends — using precisely those weaves. Not one more, not one less.

Multiplayer (The Duel)

Two or more Druids gather around a shared device. One player starts a Duel session and adds all participants. When ready, the challenge is revealed simultaneously to all players. Each Druid weaves independently, racing to be the first to complete a closed, valid knot using the exact set of weaves specified. The first to complete calls it, and the group verifies — then the winner is recorded and a photo of the winning knot can be saved for posterity.

The Five Weaves

Loop

Quickly ties up loose ends. Turns paths back on themselves.

Span

Bridges distance. Good for connecting far-apart strands.

Bend

Redirects wandering paths. Pulls strands back together.

Branch

Opens or closes paths. Can expand or contract the knot.

Cross

Works well on the inside of a Knot. Interlaces strands.

Completing a Challenge

A knot is complete when every strand is part of a closed loop — no strand has a loose end dangling free. You must use all of the specified weaves and no others. When your knot is complete, tap the Stop Timer button, then Mark Complete to record your time.

Challenges

Challenges are rated Easy, Medium, or Hard based on their composition. Easier challenges use more Bends and fewer complex weave types. Harder challenges introduce Spans and Crosses, which require more planning to close the knot.

The Lore of the Knot

The Guild of the Knot

Beneath the whispering oaks of ancient Ériu, there exists a circle older than memory — the Guild of the Knot. These men and women, known as Druids, do not merely practice weaving as craft. They understand, as few do, that the patterns they create are tethers between worlds — stitching together wind and water, stone and root, life and spirit.

Each Knot the Guild weaves is an act of balance. The land sings when a Knot is completed correctly. It shivers when one is left unfinished. The Druids take this responsibility seriously, gathering by firelight to work in concert, their breath fogging in the chill of Samhain and Beltane alike.

The Morrigan

The Morrigan is keeper of thresholds and timing. She watches the crossings where strands meet. Shapeshifter, crow-goddess, the great queen who walks the edge between order and chaos — she does not weave herself, but her breath moves through every Knot. She whispers the shape of things that should be. She nudges. She tests.

A Knot completed in her name becomes more than pattern — it becomes a ward. A promise. A seal pressed into the fabric of the world. The Morrigan rewards those who keep their commitments to the Knot and remembers those who do not.

The Five Weaves

Long ago, the first Druids did not know how to weave. The Morrigan came to them in the form of a crow and dropped five tiles at their feet — Loop, Span, Bend, Branch, and Cross. "These are the letters," she said, "of a language older than yours. Learn them. Build with them. Close what you open." Then she was gone.

The Druids studied the tiles for seven years before they wove their first closed Knot. When the last strand locked into place, a sound rang across the valley like a harp string plucked by no human hand. The oak trees bent. The rivers paused. The Morrigan, watching from a high branch, ruffled her feathers and said nothing, but her eyes were satisfied.

The Duel

The oldest tradition of the Guild is the Duel — not a contest of violence, but of speed and clarity. Two or more Druids are given the same Knot and race to complete it first. The victor's Knot is placed at the center of the circle as an offering. The others are studied, discussed, and in their imperfections, learned from.

The Duel is never truly about winning. It is about sharpening the mind against another mind — seeing how differently two people can solve the same puzzle, find the same shape. "There is only one closed Knot," the saying goes, "but a thousand paths to it."

Naming the Knot

When a Knot is sealed, the Guild pauses. Together, they give it a name — a thing remembered. In the ancient days, a scribe would sketch the pattern and record it on vellum in the Guild's great chronicle. The record served not merely as history but as proof: here is what we made, here is when, here is who was present. The Morrigan keeps her own record, of course, but the Guild keeps theirs.

Today the chronicle lives in this app. Photograph your knot. Name it if you like. Let it be remembered.
